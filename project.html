James Connolly

Net-zero targets are often a central part of national policy to negate global warming and subsequently limit global temperatures to 1.5˚C. By balancing greenhouse gas emissions with the amount removed from the atmosphere through carbon removal and sequestration, you have achieved Net-zero. This project will provide a background for the need of Net-zero Targets, whilst uncovering the lack of transparency and various misconceptions embedded within current national targets. I will then address the subsequent impact of "in-sufficient" targets.

Over the past 30 years, we’ve seen a substantial rise in CO₂ emissions, especially in parts of Asia and Africa. To highlight this, I obtained data from the EIA’s API. Each countries data was stored in an individual API, so I looped over a list of country codes I scraped from a table on IBAN’s website, which I then combined into a singular dataframe. The API only included country codes, so I imported pycountry on Python to match the Alpha-3 codes to the names. I then manually changed various country names to match those in the topojson to ensure no data was lost. Yearly data was stored under a ‘data’ field, so I added the data in an array. China’s CO₂ emissions are substantially higher than other countries, so I used a mid-domain scale in Vega-lite to balance the maps colours.

Countries Net-zero Target’s often vary. Many countries have ambitious targets (2030), but no carbon sequestration plans or reporting mechanisms, and many targets are only pledges. I used a REST API from Net Zero Tracker to gather this data. I again matched country names to those in the topojson. I had to call a significant number of API’s, so in the case of an error, a message would be printed. Most countries have some form of target, which varies in its mechanism. However side-by-side, the maps highlight the growth of emissions in countries like India and China, which worryingly have distant targets.

Carbon capture and storage is essential to reaching net-zero, however we’ve seen that many countries have no removal strategy. Chart 3 depicts the need for CCS, especially in countries with greater GHG emissions. Many smaller countries have little CCS development and subsequently a greater need to deploy it, given the cost barrier and technology of direct air capture. Countries can plant trees, however it’s land intensive and not an overarching solution. I imported linregress within Python, to calculate the regression line. CCS data only existed within an online map, so I manually typed a csv to gather the data. I imported math into Python to log GHG, to compress the y-axis.

The level of environmental protection spending as detailed within target plans can often vary. To highlight this, I used protection spending from an IMF’s API. Although all the necessary data was within a single-API call, the relevant data was within an ‘attributes’ field which was within a ‘features’ field. I therefore had to loop through the features and extract the 'attributes' field from each feature, which was appended. The IMF and tracker data were in different orders, so I merged on ISO3 values.

The legal status of targets is often overlooked and subsequently dictates the sufficiency of targets. Countries with no or proposed targets emit less, suggesting their smaller and/or LDC’s. I grouped the API data together by legal status and calculated a mean of GHG emissions and target year to match the bar-chart format. To make the no-target data visible, I coded the target year as 3000, which I then removed in the vega-lite specification.

I performed Principal Component Analysis to analyse whether the GHG emissions and carbon capture requirement of a country can identify the Net-zero Target year. Countries with 2000/2030 targets and 2045/2050 are clustered closely together, suggesting closely timed targets are correlated as expected. This data was taken from my tracker API.

If current policies lack transparency or detailed plans, how will this effect future GHG emissions? I used csv data taken from the climate action tracker, given that API projection data is not available. I converted my data into a dictionary, then into a list of its values and then wrote the string of that list to a file in JSON format. Chart 7 highlights the need to strengthen targets and even change many 2050 targets to 2030, if we wish to meet the 1.5˚C target.

Conclusions

'

It’s clear that there are many elements which dictate the sufficiency of a net-zero target. Many rapidly expanding economies have distant targets, with an increasing need for CCS technology and greater environmental protection spending. If the technology to meet net-zero through sequestration is currently not available or too expensive, countries should not use these targets on the bet that a carbon capture process with save the day in the future.
